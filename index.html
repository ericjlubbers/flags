Echoes of the Confederacy

Seven U.S. state flags share notable elements of the Confederacy's national and battle flags. Compare the major Confederate flags with the current state flags of Alabama, Arkansas, Florida, Georgia, Tennessee, North Carolina and Mississippi.

CURRENT STATE FLAGS

  • Alabama

    Adopted: 1895 Alabama adopted their state flag, which is a red St. Andrew's cross, which was also used as a reference for the Confederate battle flag, on a field of white, in 1895.

  • Arkansas

    Adopted: 1913 A local DAR chapter, after discovering Alabama had no official state flag, held a contest to design the flag in 1912. The three stars below the name represent France, Spain and the United States, all countries Arkansas had belonged to, and the fourth star above the name was added right before ratification to represent the Confederate States of America.

  • Florida

    Adopted: 1900 Florida's flag, like Alabama's, is based on the St. Andrew's cross, but incorporates the state's official seal.

  • Georgia

    Adopted: 2002 From 1956-2001, the Georgia flag contained the state seal on blue in the "hoist" (the left third of the flag), flanked by the Confederate battle flag on the "fly" (the 2/3 of the flag on the right) (take a look). In 2001, a complicated new flag design was adopted, but widely rejected and in 2003, the current version was ratified, despite critics saying the flag is based on the "Stars and Bars," the first official Confederate flag.

  • Tennessee

    Adopted: 1905 Designed by a Colonel in the Tennessee reserve in 1905, there is no direct evidence that the flag was intentionally designed with any reference to the flags of the Confederacy, writes vexillologist Steven A Knowlton, in a 2013 article. He does highlight several similar design elements, however, including the white stars and specifically the vertical bar, which he wrote is reminiscent of the vertical bar of the third national flag of the Confederacy.

  • North Carolina

    Adopted: 1885 North Carolina's flag is the oldest on this list, and is very similar to the flag the state adopted immediately after secession from the Union in 1861.

  • Mississippi

    Adopted: 1894 The Mississippi flag is the most directly related to the Confederacy, with a full CSA battle flag flanked by three stripes in the same layout as the CSA's first national flag (though the top stripe is blue, not red).
